Technology Risks


There are many risks for implementation of AI into cancer diagnosis, Because of the information required for training for effective AI recognition algorithms many patients may need to be required to volunteer or to test on for accurate cancer diagnosis. After algorithms have been trained the increased digitalisation makes hospitals more dependent on electricity which could be dangerous during blackout emergencies. The infrastructure AI requires such as servers or steady internet access will also be costly. Continuous costs such as maintenance and monetisation from private companies of such models could put heavy strain on the already overstretched state healthcare budget. It could further widen health inequalities based on wealth or location favouring either rich or city populations. The lack of generalizability of the data could be prevalent, AI trained on data from one set of the population could cause false diagnoses in other population groups, such as people from different ethnicities, age groups, or places with differing amounts of funding and available medical equipment. General trust from the population will also be an issue. Under the New Zealand Bill of Rights Act “Everyone has the right to refuse to undergo any medical treatment.”, this will also extend to treatment using AI systems causing hospitals to not only see limited usage of a costly treatment but will also worsen the access to training data thus reducing the effectiveness for the entire population.
