The impact of Artificial Intelligence on Survival Rates in Early Detection of Lung Cancer



Abstract

Lung cancer is the most commonly diagnosed cancer type. Because the survival rate is closely linked to how soon the cancer is found, most people not showing symptoms until stage 3 (Sandeep, Ramakrishnan, & Murali, 2024), and early-stage lung cancer is difficult for even professionals to detect (Choudhury et al., 2025). The healthcare industry is working to implement AI to help diagnose lung cancer earlier.
There are lots of opportunities to implement AI for lung cancer diagnosis. Some of the more notable opportunities include early detection, increased survival rate of cancer patients, and more. For example, implementing AI to help lung cancer detection could help diagnose lung cancer earlier and more accurately compared to traditional methods (Leonard et al., 2020). This is because AI can find patterns in massive amounts of data that humans are not able to (Li et al., 2022). By detecting lung cancer earlier, it would increase lung cancer survival rate since lung cancer is only so deadly because it is often detected too late for any effective treatment (Choudhury et al., 2025). Furthermore, early detection of lung cancer will also increase the affordability of treatment, lessening the financial burden on patients and their families.
There are also potential risks that are involved with the introduction of AI for lung cancer diagnosis. Overdiagnosis is a potential risk that we could see since there was overdiagnosis with LDCT screening, and AI-assisted screening showed a higher sensitivity, which could mean that AI-based screening would increase the risk of overdiagnosis. The next risk is wider health inequalities, as AI may only be cost-effective if it costs a low amount in a pay-per-case setting or receives frequent use (Du et al., 2023), which lower-income healthcare systems may not be able to achieve. Additionally, implementation challenges are frequent overlooked (Istasy et al., 2022), which may potentially result in low-income healthcare systems being unable to implement AI. Finally, AI-based models were found to have an increased bias when compared to regression models, and bias can occur at different stages (Sourlos et al., 2022), all of which may increase the risk false-positives for underrepresented groups.
Overall, there are lots of challenges involving lung cancer detection AI, as a result many choices will have to be made in order to overcome them. For example, one of the challenges would be privacy and data collection. By complying with different regulations, the accuracy of AI will be reduced due to random data and more (Vota et al., 2025). On the other hand, not complying with regulations and prioritizing data will violate privacy and cause distrust in the AI lung cancer detection technologies among the community. As a result, a choice must be made in the specification of regulation and the direction of advancement to improve AI detection. There are also many other choices that must be made because of the challenges that come with introducing AI that detects lung cancer into the medical and health industry.
